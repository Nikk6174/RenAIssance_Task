1.  EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We present RenAIssance — a fully automated, multi-stage OCR pipeline purpose-
built for digitising 17th-century Spanish printed texts. The system achieves an
average Character Error Rate (CER) of 5–7% across diverse manuscript pages,
with an accent-normalised CER of approximately 3%. On clean, well-preserved
pages the pipeline achieves sub-1% CER. These results represent a 63%
reduction over EasyOCR (18.92%) and a 47% reduction over PyTesseract (11.33%)
on identical inputs — while the reference pipeline starts at 34.75% raw CER
before any correction.

The pipeline introduces three key innovations:

  1. O(N) Autocorrelation-Based Line Detection — replacing conventional O(N²)
     hierarchical clustering with a signal-processing approach that self-
     calibrates to any font size, zoom level, or scan resolution.

  2. Needleman-Wunsch Dynamic Alignment — a novel application of bioinformatics
     sequence-alignment algorithms to automatically match OCR predictions with
     ground-truth lines, handling insertions, deletions, and shifted detections
     without manual annotation.

  3. Multi-Stage Correction with Anti-Hallucination Guards — a four-stage
     correction pipeline (TrOCR → Rule-Based → T5 → Gemini) with domain-
     specific guards that prevent LLMs from over-correcting archaic spellings.

All models are publicly available on Hugging Face. The interactive Streamlit
evaluation app auto-downloads them on first launch — no configuration needed.

2.  THE CHALLENGE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Historical Spanish manuscripts present a unique combination of challenges that
render off-the-shelf OCR systems fundamentally inadequate:

  • Orthographic Instability: Letters u/v are used interchangeably ("vno" = 
    "uno"), as are f/s ("fervir" = "servir"). No modern OCR model is trained
    on these alternations.

  • Archaic Characters: The cedilla (ç) appears where modern Spanish uses z
    ("hazer" → "haçer"). Accent marks are inconsistently applied or entirely
    absent.

  • Variable Typography: Line spacing, font weight, and ink density vary not
    just between documents, but within a single page — rendering fixed-
    parameter line detection unreliable.

  • Degraded Source Material: Ink bleed-through, foxing, faded characters, and
    uneven margins produce noise that confuses thresholding algorithms.

These challenges make it impossible to simply "run Tesseract" on the scans.
A purpose-built pipeline with domain-specific pre-processing, fine-tuned
recognition models, and historically-aware post-correction is required.

4.  KEY INNOVATIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

4.1  O(N) Autocorrelation-Based Line Detection
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Conventional approaches to text-line segmentation rely on connected-component
analysis followed by hierarchical agglomerative clustering to group components
into lines. This has O(N²) time complexity in the number of components and
requires manual tuning of distance thresholds for each document type.

Our approach:
  1. Compute the horizontal projection profile H(y) — a 1D signal of ink
     density at each row. Cost: O(W × H), a single image scan.
  2. Apply autocorrelation (ACF) to H(y) to detect the dominant periodicity,
     yielding the line period (font height + inter-line gap) automatically.
  3. Use the estimated period to place cut-lines at valley positions in the
     smoothed projection, segmenting lines in a single O(H) pass.

The result is a fully self-calibrating detector that adapts to any font size,
zoom level, or scan resolution without manual parameter tuning — and runs in
O(N) time where N is the image height.

Fallback hierarchy:
  Priority 1:  Autocorrelation (ACF) — most accurate, ≥ 4 lines
  Priority 2:  Connected-component 75th-percentile height — fewer lines
  Priority 3:  Image-height heuristic — last resort (single-line images)


4.2  Needleman-Wunsch Dynamic Ground-Truth Alignment
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A critical but often overlooked problem in OCR evaluation is: how do you know
which detected line corresponds to which ground-truth line? Naïve 1-to-1
mapping breaks catastrophically when the detector finds extra boxes (titles,
ornaments, noise) or misses a line — every subsequent pairing shifts, inflating
the measured CER by up to 60 percentage points.

We solved this by adapting the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm (originally designed
for DNA sequence alignment in bioinformatics) to OCR evaluation:

  • Predictions and GT lines are treated as two "sequences" to be aligned.
  • A dynamic-programming matrix finds the optimal global alignment that
    minimises total character edit distance across the entire document.
  • Extra detected boxes are marked as SKIPPED (insertions).
  • Missing GT lines are handled as GAPS (deletions).
  • A windowed variant (window = ±5 lines) further reduces the search space.

Complexity: O(N × M × W) where N = predictions, M = GT lines, W = window size.
In practice, W ≪ M, making it effectively O(N × W) — linear in document length.

This innovation alone corrected our measured CER from 61% (broken mapping) to
7% (true performance), revealing that TrOCR was already far more accurate than
initial evaluations suggested.


4.3  Domain-Specific Rule-Based Correction
─────────────────────────────────────────────

Before invoking any neural model, we apply deterministic, linguistically-
motivated corrections that are provably safe (zero risk of hallucination):

  Rule                        Logic                          Source
  ─────────────────────────── ───────────────────────── ──────────────────
  ç → z (unconditional)       100% of ç in 17th-c.     Historical norm
                               Spanish corresponds to
                               modern z
  u ↔ v resolution            Check against a 1,763-    Corpus dictionary
                               word archaic dictionary
  f ↔ s resolution            Same dictionary lookup    Corpus dictionary
  Accent normalisation        Strip diacritics before   Evaluation metric
                               CER computation

The dictionary was automatically compiled from the training corpus plus an
embedded vocabulary list of archaic Spanish word forms. This ensures that the
corrections are grounded in the actual language of the documents, not modern
Spanish assumptions.


4.4  Multi-Stage LLM Correction with Anti-Hallucination Guards
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We evaluated two LLMs for post-OCR correction:

  T5 (Local, Fine-Tuned)
    • Model: vgaraujov/t5-base-spanish-spellchecker, fine-tuned on 552 real
      TrOCR prediction-ground truth pairs + 2,340 synthetic error pairs +
      468 identity pairs (to teach "do not change correct text").
    • Training: 10 epochs, batch size 4, gradient accumulation 8, AdamW with
      LR 3e-5, label smoothing 0.1, early stopping patience 3.
    • Innovation: Trained on REAL TrOCR errors (not just synthetic noise),
      using the actual output of our TrOCR model on training images.

  Gemini 2.5 Flash (API)
    • Model: Google Gemini 2.5 Flash via API.
    • Prompting: Ultra-conservative few-shot prompt with explicit examples
      demanding preservation of archaic spellings.
    • Temperature: 0.0 (fully deterministic).

  Anti-Hallucination Guards:
    • Temperature = 0 for both models.
    • Word-length monitoring to detect and reject over-corrections.
    • Few-shot prompting (Gemini) with explicit "do NOT modernise" examples.
    • Per-stage CER tracking to objectively measure whether each correction
      stage helps or hurts — eliminating guesswork.


4.5  Accent-Normalised CER Metric
──────────────────────────────────

Standard CER penalises the model for producing correctly accented modern Spanish
(e.g., "también") when the 17th-century source text omitted the accent
("tambien"). This is a measurement artefact, not a model error.

We introduced accent_norm_cer: a modified CER computation that strips Unicode
combining characters (á→a, é→e, í→i, ó→o, ú→u, ñ→n) before computing edit
distance. This metric accurately reflects the model's true transcription
quality on historical texts where accentuation is fundamentally inconsistent.

7.1  Cross-Century Generalisation
    • Extend the pipeline to 15th–18th century manuscripts across multiple
      Romance languages (Portuguese, Italian, French).
    • Transfer-learn TrOCR on period-specific corpora with curriculum learning
      (easy pages first, degraded pages later).

7.2  Confidence-Gated LLM Correction
    • Implement a confidence estimator that only sends low-confidence lines to
      T5/Gemini, preserving high-confidence TrOCR output untouched.
    • Expected to reduce T5 regression to zero while still fixing hard cases.

7.3  Active Learning Pipeline
    • Use the Streamlit app's per-line CER data to identify the hardest lines.
    • Feed those lines back into TrOCR fine-tuning for iterative improvement —
      a self-improving loop that gets better with every evaluation run.

7.4  Layout-Aware Detection
    • Extend from single-column to multi-column, marginalia, and footnote
      layouts using a lightweight detection head (YOLO-based).
    • Preserve the O(N) line segmentation within each detected region.

7.5  End-to-End PDF Pipeline
    • Accept raw PDF scans and produce structured, searchable output with
      page-level metadata, confidence scores, and correction provenance.
    • Enable large-scale digitisation of entire archival collections.

7.6  Benchmarking & Open Datasets
    • Release a standardised benchmark for historical Spanish OCR with diverse
      document types, degradation levels, and typographic styles.
    • Enable fair comparison across systems and track progress over time.

RenAIssance achieves 5–7% average CER on 17th-century Spanish manuscripts,
with sub-1% on clean pages and approximately 3% when accent-normalised. The
system's innovations in O(N) self-calibrating line detection, bioinformatics-
inspired alignment, and domain-specific rule correction combine to produce a
pipeline that is not only more accurate than competing approaches but also
more robust, faster, and fully reproducible.

The 63% CER reduction over EasyOCR and 47% reduction over PyTesseract
demonstrate that the key to historical OCR is not a better general-purpose
model, but a purpose-built system that understands the specific orthographic,
typographic, and linguistic characteristics of its target domain.

By open-sourcing our models on Hugging Face and providing an interactive
Streamlit evaluation interface, we ensure that this work is immediately
usable by digital humanities researchers, archivists, and historians
working with early modern Spanish printed sources.
